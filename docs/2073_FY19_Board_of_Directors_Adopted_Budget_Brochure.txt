THE SCHOOLS
RSU5 is built from the communities of Durham, Freeport and Pownal. Freeport High School regularly
scores in the top 10% of all Maine high schools on standardized tests. We encourage our students to
look beyond those numbers and create and embrace their own learning goals.
RSU5 schools serve 1,947 students in grades PreK through grade 12. The district operates six schools,
specifically a PreK-5 Elementary School in Pownal, a PreK-8 school in Durham, and PreK-2, 3-5, 6-8 and
9-12 schools in Freeport.

ACCOUNTABILITY INFORMATION
The Elementary and Secondary Education Act (ESEA), formerly known as the No Child Left Behind
(NCLB) Act, requires all states, school districts, and schools to provide annual report cards to parents
and communities. Maine’s ESEA Report Cards include Maine Educational Assessment (MEA) data
disaggregated into subgroups, additional accountability indicators (e.g., graduation rates), and
information about teacher qualifications. This report card also displays statewide academic
achievement results in grades four and eight on the National Assessment of Educational Progress
(NAEP) reading and mathematics assessments. Please visit www.rsu5.org/administration2/28 to review
the report cards.
DISTRICT SCORECARD
The RSU Scorecard provides information on academic achievement and academic growth throughout
the district. RSU5 uses the MAP Growth assessment often referred to as the NWEA (Northwest
Evaluation Association) assessment, a research-based computerized assessment, as a measure to
determine academic growth and academic achievement in literacy and math across the District in
grades 3-10. Please visit www.rsu5.org/administration2/28 to review the report cards.

Morse Street School
by Julie Nickerson
Last year at Morse Street School, we focused on strengthening
our support for early intervention and RTI (Response to Intervention).
We welcomed 48 pre-k students through our doors in September. We
now offer three part time sessions of pre-k. This was made possible
by the addition of a 0.6 pre-k teacher and a 0.6 educational technician
which was approved in last year’s budget. Pre-k classes provide a
more balanced start to kindergarten by teaching readiness skills in
literacy and numeracy as well as social-emotional development and
familiarity with the school community as a whole. We feel fortunate
t o offer this to families. Registration for students starting pre-k in 2018 is currently underway.
The addition of a math RTI (Res ponse to Intervention) teacher has enhanced our intervention
team so we can fully support students’ academic needs. The math RTI teacher provides targeted
math int ervention to those student s who need the additional support. The RTI support ensures that
students are meeting proficiency from the onset of their K-12 journey, and ensures that
foundational skills are strong. This lays the groundwork for students to meet proficiency as work
demands increase throughout the schools.
This year, continuing to work on strengthening our RTI support in school, we are requesting the
addition of 0.2 social work time. This will enable us to provide more targeted and sustained support
to students with behavioral or social-emotional needs. This time will be directed towards
supporting students in regular education through collaborative development and implementation of
support plans with classroom teachers. This request is matched at Mast Landing School and the
proposal enables sustained support as students transition between the two schools.
Additionally, the budget increases three current regular educational technician hours from 30
hours per week to 32.5 hours per week and replaces one ed tech II position to an ed tech III position.
These additional hours enable those employees to supervise students during arrival and dismissal
times as well as provide opportunities to collaborate with teaching staff. Increasing one current
regular ed tech II position to an ed tech III position enables that employee to engage in valuable
planning for instruction which maximizes the impact of the position and therefore support for our
students.
Mast Landing School
by Emily Grimm
O ver the course of the past year, Mast Landing School staff have worked to strengthen the school
culture and climate through implementation of the Responsive Classroom framework, “a way of
teaching that emphasizes social, emotional, and academic growth in a strong school community”
through the domains of engaging academics, positive community, effective management, and
developmental awareness (Center for Responsive Schools). Teachers deepened their understanding of
these practices through the lens of their language and proactive strategies to prevent misbehavior.
Now in its second year, they have also continued utilizing the daily Morning Meeting to build strong
classroom communities and provide students opportunities to develop social and emotional skills.
To further support efforts to enrich the learning experience for our students, staff have explored
additional pathways for students to expand and apply their learning in new environments. These

teachers to support these students in the classroom setting.
Pownal Elementary School
by Lisa Demick
Last year’s budget priorities for Pownal Elementary School focused on early intervention. We
restructured our programming for our youngest students by implementing all day kindergarten and
shifting prekindergarten to two full days a week. This has met with great success. Student
engagement in school activities, growth in academic achievement, and more time for exploration and
play are some of the benefits we are reaping.
To be able to achieve well, some students need additional social
emotional support at school. Realigning physical education and
guidance gave us the opportunity to hire a .5 guidance counselor. Her
experience and passion for helping students develop self-regulation
skills has been beneficial in creating a Care Team. This team works with
families and teachers to help students build skills to better access their
academics.
This year’s budget requests focus on support for the increase to
district maintenance and capital improvement funds. We hope to
accelerate some of the school improvement items on the district five
year plan. PES requests include upgrading all school doorknobs,
reconfiguring the main office, replacing the aging stage curtain,
resurfacing the basketball court, and purchasing a generator.

Our FHS budget for 2018-19 prioritizes providing high quality instructional support for students as
well as professional development support for teachers in order to continuously refine instructional
practices. One of our main priorities is to provide targeted support for math instruction, as that is an
area in need of growth. We are asking for a 0.5 math coaching position for grades 9-12, which is directly
linked to the goal of improved teacher effectiveness. The funds requested will directly support
professional development for math teachers, allowing us to build upon the district’s investment in the
Big Ideas Math Curriculum while providing rigorous instruction and improving student achievement in
math. In addition to a math coaching position, we are requesting stipends for our FHS department
chairs. These stipends will support our teacher leaders in helping plan, implement, and reflect on
instructional methods and student support strategies with their department members as well as
maintain logistics for each department. Finally, the proposed budget requests funds to support our
NEASC accreditation process, which will occur in the fall of 2018.
These priorities and budget requests align closely with the district’s strategic goals and will directly
support continuous improvement of instructional practices as well as increased student achievement at
FHS.
